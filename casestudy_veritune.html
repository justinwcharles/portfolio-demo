Veritune

2023

Project Manager

Team

Time

3 weeks

Tools

Figma

Brief

Inspired by recent innovations in augmented and mixed reality, our goal for this project was to create an app that utilizes visual inputs to deliver a solution to the user based on what they see.

Veritune is an app that uses AR technology to "see" your surroundings and plays music that fits the scene.

Problem

There is a strong relationship between the environment and the music people listen to, but finding the right song to match the moment can be challenging.

How might we create a solution that delivers the right tune at the right time?

Solution

Use multiple inputs such as a view of the user's surroundings, weather, mood, and heartbeat to play the perfect songs. We designed a no-nonsense experience that relieves the user of having to search for and select music with an interface that pushes everything out of users' line of sight so they can carry on with their day while leaving the app open.

Additional to designing for AR devices, we also designed a mobile app to ensure we reached a large audience.

Discovery & Definition

We began with a survey where we heard from 65 music enthusiasts between 18-35 years old. We then conducted 5 semi-structured interviews with men and women between the ages of 24-33 who listen to music daily.

From this research, we gained some key insights:

  • 43% would use this app on mobile, 15% would use an AR wearable, and 34% would use both. This informed our decision to design for both mobile and an AR wearable.
  • 97% say music impacts your feelings towards the environment, and 83% say location impacts the music you listen to. This tells us there is a strong relationship between your surroundings and the music you listen to.
  • People struggle to find songs that satisfy them in the moment, and are often not in the mood to spend time looking at a device scrolling through endless playlists. This influenced us to forego a playlist approach and instead go with an app that plays music automatically based on multiple inputs.
  • It would be cool to see where friends are and what they are listening to, so we included a friends feature.

Once we had a stronger understanding of our user, we made a list of potential features for the app. Then we plotted them on a feature prioritization matrix to gain clarity on what would have the biggest impact and would be the least complex to implement for a minimum viable product.

The key features we deduced from our research included:

  • Scanning or "seeing" the environment
  • A mood selector
  • Weather
  • Heartbeat via smart watch integration
  • Account creation, add friends
  • Ability to link Spotify & Appple Music
  • Thumbs up or down the current song
  • Pin a song to a location, and see top pins from others in a given location

With the main direction and core features of the app defined, we created a sitemap to plan the information architecture. It became clear that the sign up/onboarding flow and the home screen with the core features of the app should be our primary focus.

Lo-Fi Design

To start the visual design process, we sketched out some iterations to quickly get some ideas out. This was our jumping off point to compare and discuss some different ideas.

We then created some wireframes and began user testing, focusing on the home screen and onboarding flow. We used mobile wireframes for testing because we wanted to ensure we could include all the features we were aiming for with smaller screen real estate while still achieving a minimalist design.

Users preferred a combination of the 2 home screen iterations

Home Interation 1 showed status icons for weather, mood, and heart rate with each icon expanding independently. Iteration 2 showed collapsed dashboards that expanded to show a weather, mood, and heart rate dashboard all at once.

Users wanted to see the icons showing the status of weather, mood, and heart rate, but to be able to expand the dashboard to see everything all at once.

There was a simple tweak to how users wanted to select choices during onboarding

They wanted to be able to select multiple options in a button format, rather than forcing a single selection in a drop down menu.

Hi-Fi Design

With our lo-fi design and testing complete, it was time to define the visual style and move into hi-fi. To start, we held a brainstorming session where each group member contributed ideas to a mood board we thought would influence our design:

  • Purple palette to invoke feelings of creativity, mystery, and magic
  • Glassmorphism approach with transparent windows
  • Maps view for the friends and pins features
  • Video game HUD-style approach to the UI

Using the mood board as inspiration, we created the following style guide, which went through several iterations throughout the design process.

Our color palette was very simple. White was ideal for text considering the background will be ever-changing based on what the user is looking at. Gradients were used for buttons and cards. The cards featured strong opacity and blur values to achieve a glassmorphism feel while keeping everything legible.

Research on design patterns for AR wearable devices informed our decision to create separate style guides in some areas for mobile and AR wearables

One difference was typography. For AR wearables, text is more legible for the user with heavier font weights.

Another difference between mobile and AR wearables is the size of touch targets. We used 44 pts as the standard for mobile and 60 pts as the standard for AR wearables. Icons are text were also scaled appropriately.

The biggest difference was the grid system. When it comes to AR wearables, there is a sweet spot where it is most comfortable for users to move their eyes and head to view content. It is advisable to design horizontally rather than vertically, but with a limit on how far to the left and right you should go. This was very important for our design since our goal was to push the UI away from the center as far as possible.

For mobile, we made sure to leave space at the top and bottom for the mobile operating system UI.

The style guide was utilized to transform the lo-fi mockups into a near-finished product. Group members were assigned different parts of the site to work on, and then I reviewed and updated each frame to ensure a consistent look and feel through the app.

This is the progression from sketches to wireframes to hi-fi.

With the look and feel of the app becoming nearly complete, we did one last round of testing to ensure everything was making sense to the user

As we moved to hi-fi, we made the decision to forego a menu-style navigation, and instead allow the user to swipe left and right to navigate to other parts of the app. A settings button was placed where the menu was previously.

Users were not clear on how to use and navigate the app, so we added a tutorial after the onboarding flow.

Finally, we put the finishing touches on the design and programmed working prototypes for AR wearables and mobile

We were pleased to use a video I captured at a local coffee shop to simulate what a user would see if they were wearing a mixed reality headset or using their mobile device's camera. From the home screen, I love how the left and right swiping animation looks on top of the looping background video.


Retro-
spective

We set ourselves up for success by spending time on research and testing throughout the project

We were able to start in a clear direction and then learn critical feedback along the way before we invested too much time in hi-fi design. Additionally, researching about design guidelines for recent AR wearable technology was interesting and played a big part in how we designed the style guide.

Given the time constraints we were working under, we quickly moved into hi-fi design before the style guide was completed

This caused some rework at the end to ensure a consistent look and feel across the app. My biggest takeaway from this project was to ensure a robust style guide is in place and to communicate clearly across the team about how to use it before moving to hi-fi design.

This was such a fun project to work on! We worked well as a team and learned a lot along the way.
